# Nouns - Possessive, Collective, and Abstract

## Content
A noun is a naming word. It names a person, place, animal, or thing.

**1. Possessive Nouns:**
A possessive noun shows ownership or belonging. It is formed by adding an apostrophe (') or apostrophe + s ('s).

Rules:
- Singular noun + 's: boy → boy's book
- Plural noun ending with s + ': boys → boys' school
- Plural noun not ending with s + 's: children → children's park

Examples:
- Rohan's bicycle (The bicycle belongs to Rohan)
- The teacher's desk (The desk belongs to the teacher)
- The girls' uniforms (The uniforms belong to the girls)
- The children's toys (The toys belong to the children)

**2. Collective Nouns:**
A collective noun names a group of people, animals, or things.

Examples:
- A **team** of players
- A **class** of students
- A **family** of four
- A **flock** of birds
- A **herd** of cows
- A **swarm** of bees
- A **bunch** of grapes
- A **bouquet** of flowers
- A **fleet** of ships
- An **army** of soldiers

**3. Abstract Nouns:**
An abstract noun names something we cannot see, touch, hear, smell, or taste. It is an idea, feeling, or quality.

Examples:
- Love (feeling)
- Happiness (feeling)
- Bravery (quality)
- Kindness (quality)
- Friendship (relationship)
- Honesty (quality)
- Peace (state)
- Freedom (state)

## Key Points
- Possessive nouns show ownership (add 's or s')
- Collective nouns name a group
- Abstract nouns name ideas and feelings we cannot touch
- Abstract nouns answer "what" for things without physical form

## Vocabulary
- **Possessive Noun**: Shows ownership
- **Apostrophe**: Punctuation mark (') used in possessives
- **Collective Noun**: Word for a group
- **Abstract Noun**: Names ideas, feelings, or qualities
- **Concrete Noun**: Names things we can touch (opposite of abstract)

## Fun Fact
The English language has over 200 collective nouns! A group of crows is called a "murder," a group of lions is a "pride," and a group of owls is a "parliament"! 🦁
