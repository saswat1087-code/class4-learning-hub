# Subject and Predicate

## Content
Every sentence has two main parts: Subject and Predicate.

**Subject:**
The subject tells us who or what the sentence is about. It is usually a noun or pronoun.

Examples:
- The cat sleeps. (The cat = subject)
- She sings beautifully. (She = subject)
- My school is very big. (My school = subject)

**Predicate:**
The predicate tells us what the subject does or is. It contains the verb and tells something about the subject.

Examples:
- The cat sleeps. (sleeps = predicate)
- She sings beautifully. (sings beautifully = predicate)
- My school is very big. (is very big = predicate)

**Finding the Subject:**
Ask "Who?" or "What?" before the verb.

Example: "The little boy ate an apple"
- Ask: Who ate? → The little boy (subject)

**Finding the Predicate:**
The predicate is everything that is not the subject.

Example: "The little boy ate an apple"
- Predicate: ate an apple

**Simple Subject vs Complete Subject:**
- Simple Subject: Just the main noun/pronoun (boy)
- Complete Subject: All words that tell about the subject (The little boy)

**Simple Predicate vs Complete Predicate:**
- Simple Predicate: Just the verb (ate)
- Complete Predicate: Verb + all other words (ate an apple)

## Key Points
- Subject tells who or what the sentence is about
- Predicate tells what the subject does or is
- Every sentence must have a subject and a predicate
- Ask "Who?" or "What?" to find the subject
- The predicate contains the verb

## Vocabulary
- **Subject**: Who or what the sentence is about
- **Predicate**: What the subject does or is
- **Verb**: An action or state of being word
- **Simple Subject**: Just the main noun/pronoun
- **Complete Subject**: Subject with all describing words
- **Simple Predicate**: Just the verb
- **Complete Predicate**: Verb with all other words

## Fun Fact
Some sentences have an understood subject. In the command "Sit down!" the subject "you" is understood even though it's not written! 🪑
